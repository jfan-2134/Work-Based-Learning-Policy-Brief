Intended audience
Ministries of Education and Labour in lower-middle and upper-middle income countries currently developing or reforming national vocational education and training (VET) frameworks, particularly those engaged with UNICEF GenU's youth employment agenda. While the evidence in this brief draws primarily from China and Germany, the structural challenges identified including quality assurance, gender equity, and employer incentive design offer transferable lessons for governments at early or transitional stages of WBL system development.
The challenge
Why WBL matters — and where it falls short
Work-based learning (WBL) — apprenticeships, internships, and traineeships — is one of the most effective tools for bridging education and employment.[2] Yet most systems prioritize access over quality, producing placements without building genuine resilience. Globally, nearly one in five young people remain outside employment, education, or training, with school-to-work transitions becoming longer and more uncertain.[2] As digital transformation and AI reshape labor markets — with up to 40% of occupational tasks exposed to AI augmentation — WBL must evolve from a placement mechanism into a tool for long-term workforce adaptability.[1]

Case studies
Two systems, two sets of failures
🇨🇳
China — scale without alignment
Rapid expansion has prioritized volume over quality.[5,15] A ~70% wage gap between interns and regular staff incentivizes firms to cycle trainees rather than develop them,[6] while short-term retention metrics mask chronic underemployment and fabricated placement figures.[16] International returnees face a paradox: despite initial salary premiums, they experience higher early-career unemployment due to weak domestic networks.[10,11]
Labor replacement trap Inflated placement metrics Gender conversion gap Weak network access
A high-performing intern — praised as a "10-year veteran" — was not converted due to a sudden budget freeze, not a skills deficit.[16]
🇩🇪
Germany — strong foundations, growing strain
The dual apprenticeship system delivers low youth unemployment and strong employer alignment[7,8] — but faces structural pressures that limit its future effectiveness. Gender segmentation, declining youth uptake in favour of university pathways, and slow adaptation to digital skill demands expose the limits of rigid occupational design.[9]
Low youth unemployment Gender segmentation Declining youth uptake Slow digital adaptation
Strong short-term outcomes increasingly give way to long-term skill obsolescence as occupational specificity limits cross-sector flexibility.[8,9]
Gender equity
A persistent conversion gap
A 12% conversion gap for women in digital sectors reflects systemic HR biases — not a skills deficit.[3] Employers systematically treat female candidates as higher-risk investments, a dynamic amplified in China by Three-Child Policy assumptions that categorize women as liable to career interruption regardless of demonstrated performance.[3,4,20] Male candidates, by contrast, benefit from an invisible premium rooted in assumed continuity.[12]

Framework
WBL quality maturity model
A three-level diagnostic framework for GenU partners to assess, benchmark, and improve WBL program quality — shifting focus from placements to outcomes.[19]
Level 1
Survival
Access-oriented placement. Short-term, low-complexity tasks. Driven by cost efficiency. Does not ensure skill development or mobility.[18]
Metric:
placement rate
Level 2
Skills
Intentional capability-building. Structured training, mentorship, and credentials. Transferable skills beyond the immediate role.[1,6]
Metric:
skill gain rate
Level 3
Resilience
Long-term stability and adaptability. Career progression, cross-industry mobility, sustained income growth embedded in continuous learning.[2,19]
Metric:
sustained employment 12–36m
Recommendations
Four priority actions
1
Address gender bias through anonymized, competency-based conversion reviews and incentivized paternity leave to equalize perceived hiring risks.[3,13,20]
2
Prevent labor substitution via tiered employer audits — tracking intern-to-staff ratios, conversion rates by gender, and task comparisons between intern and regular staff roles.[6,16]
3
Expand bridge programs for youth lacking social capital — including international returnees and internal migrants — combining local work experience, mentorship, and structured networking.[10,11,23]
4
Embed trainee agency by actively involving trainees in goal-setting and feedback loops, ensuring WBL serves individual career progression — not just institutional metrics.[16,19]
Conclusion
WBL's promise is real — but realizing it requires moving beyond counting jobs to measuring whether young people can sustain and grow them.[2,5] Effective WBL is not defined by placements generated, but by whether it equips youth with the capabilities, networks, and adaptability to thrive in an evolving labor market.
